CT Windowing (Window/Level) Mini Tutorial

Kid-friendly idea: windowing is not “magic”. It is choosing which gray range to show clearly (like brightness + contrast).

1) What are WL and WW?

  • WL (Window Level): the “middle gray” you care about most.
  • WW (Window Width): how wide your visible gray range is. Wider = lower contrast; narrower = stronger contrast.

Easy memory: WL = center, WW = range width.

2) Why use presets?

  • Different body parts have different useful gray ranges.
  • A preset gives a fast, good starting point (so you don’t drag sliders forever).

Key point: presets change how you view the same CT. The pixels do not change.

3) A simple 3-step method (works almost every time)

  • Step A: pick the closest Preset.
  • Step B: adjust WL to make the whole image brighter/darker.
    WL up → overall brighter. WL down → overall darker.
  • Step C: adjust WW to control contrast.
    WW smaller → stronger contrast. WW larger → less contrast, fewer areas become pure black/white.

Quick analogy: WL ≈ brightness, WW ≈ contrast/sensitivity.

If you feel “I can’t see anything”: usually WW is too wide (contrast too weak) or WL is centered on the wrong tissue.

4) Common beginner mistakes

  • Brighter is not always better: too bright can wash out detail (like overexposed photos).
  • Black does not always mean a hole: lungs are naturally black (air). Bowel can contain gas too.
  • Don’t judge organs on Bone window: bone window makes soft tissue too dark.
  • A phase preset can’t change scan timing: “Arterial/Venous” are scan phases; the preset only changes the display.

5) 1-minute practice (try it now)

Practice 1: checking bones

  • Start with Bone
  • If edges look flat/washed: try a slightly smaller WW

Practice 2: lung detail

  • Start with Lung or Lung HRCT
  • If it looks too white: lower WL a bit

Reminder: this helps you “see clearly”. Diagnosis needs training and full clinical context.

Soft Tissue

  • Used for: most organs, muscles, fat.
  • Look for: organ borders, masses, inflammation, fluid (water-like areas).
  • You’ll notice: fat is often darker, muscle is mid-gray, fluid can be darker.
  • Quick tweak: not enough contrast → make WW smaller; too dark/bright overall → adjust WL.

Lung

  • Used for: lungs, airways, tiny lung textures.
  • Look for: patterns, nodules, hazy opacities, pneumothorax clues.
  • Tip: lungs are normally very black (air). Focus on “texture changes”, not black areas alone.

Bone

  • Used for: bones, fractures, spurs, calcifications.
  • Look for: bright cortex edges, cracks, step-offs, deformity.
  • Warning: soft tissues will look very dark—don’t judge organs on bone window.
  • Quick tweak: if edges look “solid white” and cracks vanish, try a slightly smaller WW or a tiny WL decrease.

Brain

  • Used for: brain parenchyma (gray/white matter).
  • Look for: symmetry, ventricle size, sulci becoming shallow (possible swelling).
  • Tip: brain differences can be subtle; small WL/WW changes can matter.

Abdomen

  • Used for: general abdominal organs (liver, spleen, kidneys, bowel).
  • Look for: organ outlines, fat planes, fluid, bowel wall thickness clues.
  • Quick tweak: start here if you’re unsure, then switch to a more specific preset.

Liver

  • Used for: liver lesions and contrast differences (especially with contrast).
  • Look for: small darker/brighter areas and whether borders are sharp.
  • Tip: liver windows are often narrower WW so small differences “pop”. If everything looks the same, reduce WW a little.

Mediastinum

  • Used for: central chest: heart area, great vessels, trachea, lymph nodes.
  • Look for: masses, lymph nodes, vessel shape, pleural fluid.

Spine

  • Used for: vertebrae and spinal canal context.
  • Look for: fractures, vertebral height, canal narrowing clues.

Pelvis

  • Used for: pelvic organs and pelvic bones together.
  • Look for: fluid/bleeding clues, bladder shape, pelvic fractures.

Head/Neck

  • Used for: head/neck soft tissues, glands, lymph nodes.
  • Look for: swelling, masses, airway narrowing clues.

CTA/Vascular

  • Used for: contrast-enhanced vessels and vessel lumen.
  • Look for: narrowing, widening, blockage (thrombus), leakage clues.
  • Tip: vessels being bright is normal with contrast. Compare the “bright tube” shape for continuity.
  • Quick tweak: if vessels are too white with no detail, increase WW a bit or raise WL slightly.

Kidney

  • Used for: renal parenchyma and collecting system area.
  • Look for: stones (often very bright), masses, hydronephrosis clues.
  • Tip: if you suspect a stone, compare with Bone to see bright foci clearly.

Pancreas

  • Used for: pancreas borders (often affected by nearby bowel/fat).
  • Look for: enlargement and hazy surrounding fat (a common inflammation clue).

Trauma

  • Used for: fast wide scan to spot “big problems” first.
  • Look for: bleeding clues, pneumothorax clues, fractures, major organ injury hints.
  • Warning: trauma is not a single organ—switch to Bone/Lung/Soft Tissue presets to confirm findings.
  • Workflow tip: do one fast pass, then check systematically with more specific windows.

Subdural

  • Used for: head trauma—looking for bleeding near the inner skull.
  • Look for: a crescent-shaped brighter area along the inner skull.
  • Tip: bone window can distract you (skull is extremely bright). This preset helps the bleed stand out more.

Stroke

  • Used for: making tiny brain density differences easier to notice.
  • Look for: one region slightly darker, blurred borders, sulci becoming less visible.
  • Reminder: early stroke can be very hard to see; professional reading is required.
  • Note: if you still can’t see changes after tuning, it may be too early or need other sequences—this is normal.

Body

  • Used for: wide general soft-tissue view across chest/abdomen/pelvis.
  • Look for: large masses, fluid, obvious asymmetry.

Extremity/MSK

  • Used for: arms/legs muscles and soft tissue swelling.
  • Look for: swelling, hematoma, and any unexpected gas bubbles (black spots) in soft tissue.

Temporal Bone

  • Used for: tiny ear-region bony structures.
  • Look for: sharp bony edges, small air cells, bone destruction clues.

Sinus

  • Used for: fluid vs air in the paranasal sinuses.
  • Look for: areas that should be black (air) becoming gray/white (fluid/mucosal thickening).

Angio Bone Sub

  • Used for: vessel-focused viewing where bone should not steal attention.
  • Look for: vessel course, narrowing, occlusion clues.
  • “Bone subtraction” is often a post-processing technique; here it may simply be a vessel-friendly window.
  • Quick tweak: if bone is still too bright, increase WW a bit or raise WL so bone is less saturated.

Lung HRCT

  • Used for: fine lung detail (ground-glass, reticulation-like textures).
  • Look for: subtle haze, fine lines, texture changes compared to nearby regions.

Orbits

  • Used for: tissues around the eyes and orbital bones.
  • Look for: fractures, swelling, bleeding clues behind the eye.

CTA Head/Neck

  • Used for: head/neck vessels in CTA.
  • Look for: stenosis, aneurysm-like bulges, occlusion clues.

Arterial

  • Used for: arterial phase studies (arteries are brightest).
  • Look for: arterial branches, narrowing, abnormal dilatation.
  • If the scan is not arterial phase, this preset won’t “make it arterial”—it only changes the display.

Venous

  • Used for: venous phase where organs enhance more evenly.
  • Look for: venous return, organ enhancement patterns, thrombus clues.

Colon/Bowel

  • Used for: bowel wall and surrounding fat planes.
  • Look for: wall thickening, hazy fat, obstruction clues.

Adrenal

  • Used for: adrenal glands (small and easy to miss).
  • Look for: tiny nodules and asymmetry.

Gallbladder

  • Used for: gallbladder and bile ducts.
  • Look for: stones (often bright), wall thickening, inflammation clues nearby.

Skin/Subcutaneous

  • Used for: skin, subcutaneous fat, soft tissue swelling patterns.
  • Look for: “hazy” fat, swelling, and unexpected tiny gas bubbles (black dots).

Cardiac

  • Used for: heart region soft tissues and pericardium.
  • Look for: pericardial fluid, overall heart shape, surrounding fluid/mass clues.
  • Note: many cardiac CT studies use special scan timing; windowing only helps visibility.

Learning note: this page is about understanding windowing. Medical diagnosis requires professional training and full clinical information.
